Category 1: Technical Execution (Does the product work?)

Score What It Looks Like Example at This Hackathon
1-3 Nothing runs. Slides only. "We ran out of time." App crashes on launch. Just a landing page. Team that spent all weekend on idea/design but never got code working
4-5 Something runs but it's a skeleton. One screen works. Hardcoded data. Visible bugs during demo. Lovable-generated app with default styling, partially working
6-7 App works end-to-end for the demo path. A few rough edges. Data is real but limited. Crashes if you go off-script. Weekend prototype that works for the happy path only
8-9 Polished, multi-screen app. Real data. Multiple features work. Handles edge cases. Feels like a real product. Team with a strong developer who built something solid
10 YOU Production-quality app. Live in TestFlight. Multiple complex features (maps, voice, marketplace). Works offline. On a real device, not a simulator. Community Moments: 56 routes, Rally Radio, The Quarry, community feed, Apple Watch — on iPhone 17 Pro
Why you score 10: No weekend prototype competes with a TestFlight app that has 40+ Swift files, peer-to-peer voice chat, and 56 GPS routes with elevation profiles. Hand the judge your phone — that's the proof.
Risk that drops you to 8: App crashes during demo. New AI features are buggy. Judge can't install TestFlight because of Apple ID issues. Mitigation: Screen recording backup. Test AI features exhaustively Saturday night. Have app running before you present.

Category 2: Problem-Solution Fit (Does it solve a real problem?)

Score What It Looks Like Example at This Hackathon
1-3 Solution looking for a problem. "We thought it would be cool." No evidence anyone needs this. Vague target audience. "AI-powered recipe app" — who asked for this?
4-5 Problem is real but generic. Solution exists elsewhere. No local angle. No validation beyond "we think people want this." "Uber for dog walking" — real problem, existing solutions
6-7 Clear problem, decent solution, but hypothetical users. "We surveyed 5 friends." No traction. No proof of demand. Good idea with a plan but no users or validation yet
8-9 Real problem with evidence. Some users or letters of intent. Clear target. Local relevance. Makes sense for Rapid City. Team that interviewed local businesses and has data
10 YOU Problem validated by a real organization. Grant money awarded. Event scheduled. Users exist. Nonprofit backing. The problem isn't hypothetical — it's being solved right now. Stone Bicycle Coalition: $1K AEP grant, Pedal for Empathy May 2, partnership with Black Hills Works, bikes already in The Quarry
Why you score 10: You have a registered LLC, a $1K grant, a scheduled community event 6 days after the hackathon, real bike inventory, and a nonprofit mission. This isn't "I think cyclists need this" — it's "we already received grant funding because cyclists need this."
Risk that drops you to 8: You focus too much on the tech and not enough on the human story. Judges don't understand why cycling in RC needs an app. Mitigation: Lead with the problem. "Where do you go if you're new to cycling here?" Make it personal.

Category 3: Innovation & Feasibility (Creative and achievable?)

Score What It Looks Like Example at This Hackathon
1-3 Clone of existing app. Nothing new. Or wildly infeasible — "we'll use blockchain to solve homelessness." Another to-do app or weather app with no twist
4-5 Somewhat novel but the AI angle feels bolted on. "We added a chatbot." Feasible but boring. Or creative but clearly can't ship. Interesting concept that doesn't feel like it'll work beyond the demo
6-7 Good use of AI. Novel combination of existing tech. Clearly feasible. But similar things exist in other domains. AI trip planner — creative but not new category
8 YOU Novel application of AI to a specific local problem. Peer-to-peer voice for cycling is unusual. AI route advisor from real GPS data is creative. Config-driven = any co-op can fork. Clearly achievable because it already exists. AI route advisor matching natural language to 56 real GPS routes. Rally Radio P2P voice. Open-source co-op toolkit. All proven feasible.
9-10 Category-defining. "I've never seen this before." Completely new approach. Makes judges say "why doesn't this exist already?" Something truly novel — hard to predict, you'll know it when you see it
Why 8 not 10: This is your weakest category. A cycling app with AI recommendations is smart but not revolutionary. Route recommendation exists (Strava, Komoot). Your edge is the local specificity + open-source co-op model + P2P voice, not the AI itself.
How to push to 9: Emphasize what's truly novel:
  • Rally Radio — no other cycling app has peer-to-peer voice that works without cell service. This is genuinely new.
  • Config-driven fork model — "Any bike co-op in America can fork this in a day." That's infrastructure, not just an app.
  • "Built entirely with AI" — Frame the development process itself as innovation. One person + Claude Code = production app. That's the vibecoding story they want to hear.
  • Weather-aware safety — "The app tells you NOT to ride." An app that sometimes says no is more trustworthy than one that always says yes.

Category 4: Demo & Presentation Quality (Can the team communicate?)

Score What It Looks Like Example at This Hackathon
1-3 Mumbling. Reading from notes. No demo, just slides. Over time. Team clearly didn't rehearse. Confusing narrative. Team where one person talks and others stand silently
4-5 Decent slides but no live demo. Or live demo that goes sideways. Team is nervous. Story doesn't connect problem to solution clearly. Good idea, mediocre delivery. Judges have to ask "so what does it actually do?"
6-7 Clear narrative. Live demo works. On time. But generic — could be any pitch competition. Team roles unclear. No emotional hook. Competent presentation that doesn't stick in your memory
8 Strong narrative arc. Live demo on device. Confident delivery. Team members each contribute. Emotional connection to the problem. On time. Well-rehearsed team that clearly practiced together
9-10 TARGET Unforgettable. Judge holds the product in their hands. Story has a beginning, middle, and end. Team reflects on what they built and learned. Closes with real impact (upcoming event, grant, users). Judges nod at each other. Hand phone to judge. 56 routes on their screen. "This is live. Pedal for Empathy is May 2. We're ready." Team each shares one thing they built.
Risk that drops you to 7: You talk too fast. You fill the whole 5 minutes yourself and teammates stand silently. Demo path hits a bug. You run over time. Mitigation: Practice 3x Sunday morning. Give each teammate a speaking part. Time it. Have the demo flow written down as a checklist.
How to guarantee 9+:
  • Hand the phone to a judge. Physical product in their hands = unforgettable. Nobody else will do this.
  • Team reflection round. Each teammate says one sentence about what they built or learned. Judges eat this up — it shows real collaboration.
  • End on impact, not features. "Pedal for Empathy is May 2. This app will be in people's hands that day." Future tense = momentum.
  • Pause at the end. Don't rush off. Say "Thank you" and stand still for 2 seconds. Confidence reads as quality.
