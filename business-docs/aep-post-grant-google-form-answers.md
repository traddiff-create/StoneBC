## 1. Brief recap of the grant project and its impact

On Saturday, May 2, 2026, Stone Bicycle Coalition partnered with the City of Rapid City Parks & Recreation, Acme Bikes, and the Minneluzahan Senior Citizen Center for *Pedal for Empathy* — a community ride, urban bike-path cleanup, and post-ride gathering in Rapid City, SD. Nine neighbors gathered at Hanson-Larsen Memorial Park for coffee and donuts at 10:30 AM, rode roughly five miles of Rapid City's path system at a social pace, filled two 42-gallon contractor bags of trash off those same paths, and wrapped up at Acme Bikes. AEP grant funds covered cleanup supplies, food and drinks for volunteers, advertising and signage, and a $350 in-person donation to Feeding South Dakota (BHCB cashier's check #36604, delivered 2026-04-30; signed acknowledgement received 2026-05-01).

## 2. Self-assessment — goals achieved, obstacles, surprises, lessons

We hit our three application-stated empathy pillars: equitable access (a free, no-experience-required ride open to all ages), environmental stewardship (~5 miles of path cleaned, two contractor bags filled), and community connection (an intergenerational morning across HLMP → bike path → Acme Bikes). Turnout matched our 9 Mobilize sign-ups; for a first-time event from a young coalition we'd expected a tight group, and that's what we got. The biggest lesson was that "ride + cleanup + donation" — which I'd worried was three different events stapled together — actually cohered as a single thing once we let it run at a community pace. Next time we'd start outreach 6–8 weeks earlier, set a second cleanup drop point so the bags don't all live on one rider's bike, and add a mid-route water stop on the longer path segments.

## 3. High-level use of grant funds

Funds were used as proposed: cleanup supplies (gloves, first-aid, trash bags, totes), food and drinks for volunteers, event signage and a small paid-social promotion, and a $350 contingency donation to Feeding South Dakota per AEP guidelines. Total reported to AEP: **$999.28 of $1,000** (the remaining $0.72 falls within the retention threshold and was absorbed by SBC's operating account). Itemized receipts — 9 of 9 — were filed via the AEP JotForm tracker between April 30 and May 4.

## 4. What made this project meaningful?

Pedal for Empathy was Stone Bicycle Coalition's first external grant of any kind, and being picked from 600+ applicants meant something to a young Rapid City coalition still building credibility. But the deeper meaning showed up on the path. Public bike paths are exactly the kind of shared resource that quietly degrades when nobody claims them — and quietly improves when nine neighbors decide to claim them for one Saturday morning. Putting a value into action — *we ride here, so we keep it clean here* — was a small, replicable model for how a small city can take care of itself. Donating $350 to Feeding South Dakota extended that "living area" framing past the bike path and into food security for the same neighbors we ride past every week.

## 5. Story or two from the project

The moment that stuck with me wasn't part of the planned program. During the cleanup, a parent who'd brought their kids on the ride told me, more or less in passing, that they were grateful their kids were *seeing what it actually takes to keep a place usable.* That single sentence — said over a half-full trash bag, on a stretch of greenway nobody officially "owns" — is the entire empathy thesis distilled. We didn't have to lecture anyone; the bags filled up, the kids saw the bags fill up, and the lesson was already done. Smaller texture from the same morning: a bike-shop owner stayed to host the wrap-up at Acme Bikes; the Parks & Rec contact thanked us for treating the corridors like ours, not theirs; and at HLMP the donuts disappeared in roughly the time it took to clip in.

## 6. Did AEP introduce humanism to or change anyone's view of humanism?

For most participants, "humanism" as a named tradition wasn't the framing they came in with — they came for a bike ride and a cleanup. But the grant being explicitly funded by the *American Humanist Association under the American Empathy Project* gave us a natural opening to explain, in plain language, that humanism is a values tradition centered on human dignity, empathy, and showing up for each other without requiring a religious frame. A few participants asked follow-up questions and were surprised to learn it was an organized national movement at all. We wouldn't claim anyone walked away a "humanist," but the project widened the doorway. People who'd never heard of AHA now associate it with something concrete that improved their own neighborhood — and that, in our experience, is how humanism gets traction in a small Western city.

## 7. How many organizers + volunteers participated? (best estimate)

**~10.** The same 9 people who registered via Mobilize served as both ride participants and cleanup volunteers, plus a Parks & Recreation liaison (Melissa Petersen) on logistics and Acme Bikes hosting the wrap-up.

## 8. How many people were served or positively impacted? (best estimate)

**~9 directly** at the ride, cleanup, and gathering; plus the meaningful indirect impact of (a) ~5 miles of cleaner Rapid City bike path used by every cyclist, walker, runner, and family afterward, and (b) the **$350 donation to Feeding South Dakota**, which their meal-distribution multiplier extends to additional neighbors in the same region.

## 9. Did you have prior experience organizing a project like this?

**Some prior experience.** Stone Bicycle Coalition is a new organization (LLC formed 2025), but the organizers have run other community projects, events, and small business operations before. *Pedal for Empathy* was our first grant-funded event under the SBC banner.

## 10. Following AEP, more or less likely to organize a service project again?

**More likely.** The AEP process was light enough that it actually freed us up to focus on the event itself, and the result has given us a template — and a story — we can carry into future grants and partnerships.

## 11. Most helpful about the AEP grant program

Three things stood out: (1) the application was proportional to the award size — we weren't asked to write a 30-page proposal for a $1,000 grant; (2) the framing around *empathy in action* gave us a clearer lens than a generic "community grant" would have, and it shaped the event in ways our team will repeat; (3) the post-grant reporting structure (receipts via JotForm, narrative via Google Form, advance preview of the questions) is genuinely thoughtful and respects grantee time. The cohort communications from Danielle Strasburger, Jake Via, and Christina Morra were warm, clear, and on-time throughout.

## 12. How could the AEP grant program be improved?

A short library of past grantee project recaps — even a paragraph each — would help new applicants calibrate scope and ambition. As a first-time grantee we had to guess at what a typical AEP-funded event looked like. Optional peer connections between current grantees in different regions could also strengthen the network effect of the program — there's a real cohort here, and a light Slack channel or quarterly Zoom would help it function as one.

## 13. How can the American Humanist Association engage more people with humanism?

By keeping the door wide and the language plain. Most people who showed up on May 2 wouldn't self-identify as humanists, but they live the values — they show up, they take care of shared spaces, they treat strangers like neighbors. Programs like AEP that fund concrete, local, non-doctrinal action are exactly the on-ramp that lets people experience humanism before they have a label for it. More of that — at small dollar amounts, with low friction, in towns and cities AHA hasn't traditionally reached — would do more than national messaging can do alone.

## 14. Other feedback, comments, or recommendations

We're grateful — full stop. The grant was the difference between an idea on a whiteboard and a real day in our community, and being selected from 600+ applicants gave a young coalition real wind. Thank you to Danielle, Jake, and Christina, and to the AEP team broadly, for making the entire process feel like a partnership rather than a transaction. We'd be glad to participate in any future grantee networking, case-study collection, alumni cohort, or follow-on opportunities — and we'll keep showing up here in Rapid City.
