# Parts Sourcing & Partnership Guide

## Overview

Bicycle cooperatives rely on a steady supply of donated bikes, parts, and materials. This guide covers how to establish and maintain partnerships with organizations that can provide these resources.

---

## Primary Sources

### 1. Police Departments

Police departments accumulate abandoned, unclaimed, and recovered stolen bicycles. This is often the single best source for bike co-ops.

**What They Have:**
- Abandoned bicycles (found/impounded)
- Unclaimed stolen bikes (after holding period)
- Bikes from arrests/seizures
- Often 50-500+ bikes per year depending on city size

**How to Approach:**

1. **Identify the right contact**
   - Property/Evidence division
   - Community liaison officer
   - Chief's office (for formal partnerships)

2. **Make your case**
   - Nonprofit/community benefit mission
   - Proper disposal solves their storage problem
   - Good PR opportunity for department
   - Bikes go to people in need

3. **Propose arrangement**
   - Regular pickup schedule (monthly/quarterly)
   - You handle all transportation
   - Provide receipt/documentation
   - Offer recognition (signage, social media, events)

**Sample Outreach Letter:**

```
[Date]

[Police Department Name]
[Address]

RE: Bicycle Donation Partnership

Dear [Chief/Property Manager],

I am writing on behalf of [Co-op Name], a nonprofit bicycle
cooperative serving [City/Region]. We provide bicycle repair
education, earn-a-bike programs for youth and low-income
residents, and accessible transportation solutions.

We would like to propose a partnership to receive abandoned
and unclaimed bicycles from your department. This arrangement
would:

- Free up your storage space
- Ensure bikes benefit the community rather than going to scrap
- Support transportation access for residents in need
- Provide positive community engagement opportunity

We would be happy to:
- Pick up bikes on a regular schedule at your convenience
- Provide documentation for your records
- Recognize the department's contribution publicly
- Invite officers to community events

I would welcome the opportunity to discuss this partnership.
Please contact me at [phone] or [email].

Thank you for your consideration.

Sincerely,
[Name]
[Title]
[Co-op Name]
```

**Partnership Agreement Elements:**
- Pickup schedule and procedures
- Minimum holding period before release (usually 90-180 days)
- Documentation requirements
- Liability release
- Recognition terms
- Contact persons on both sides
- Review/renewal terms

### 2. Universities & Colleges

College campuses generate large quantities of abandoned bikes, especially at end of academic year.

**What They Have:**
- End-of-year abandoned bikes (May/June)
- Bikes from storage cleanouts
- Impounded bikes from bike registration programs
- Often 100-300+ bikes per year at larger schools

**Key Contacts:**
- Campus police/security
- Facilities/grounds department
- Sustainability office
- Student affairs/housing
- Transportation/parking services

**Timing:**
- **Best:** May-June (end of school year)
- **Good:** December-January (winter break/semester end)
- **Ongoing:** Throughout year from impound

**Partnership Opportunities:**
- Regular donation pickups
- On-campus repair events
- Student volunteer programs
- Bike share/rental partnerships
- Earth Day/sustainability events

**Sample Partnership Proposal:**

```
UNIVERSITY BICYCLE PARTNERSHIP PROPOSAL

[Co-op Name] proposes a partnership with [University] to:

1. RECEIVE ABANDONED BICYCLES
   - Pickup of all abandoned/unclaimed bikes
   - End-of-year cleanout coordination
   - Proper documentation provided

2. PROVIDE CAMPUS SERVICES (optional)
   - Pop-up repair clinics during bike month
   - Discounted repairs for students
   - Bike safety workshops

3. RECOGNITION
   - [University] recognized as community partner
   - Social media acknowledgment
   - Annual impact report

BENEFITS TO UNIVERSITY:
- Eliminated disposal costs
- Reduced storage burden
- Sustainability metrics
- Community engagement
- Student programming opportunity

Contact: [Name, Email, Phone]
```

### 3. Local Bike Shops

Bike shops can be valuable partners or perceived competitors. Approach carefully.

**What They Have:**
- Trade-in bikes (often given $50 credit, worth more to co-op)
- Damaged/returned bikes
- Take-off parts (new bikes come with parts that get swapped)
- Overstock/closeout items
- Shop tools being retired

**Partnership Approaches:**

| Model | Description | Best For |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **Donation** | Shop donates trade-ins, take-offs | Shops wanting tax deduction |
| **Purchase** | Buy trade-ins at wholesale | Shops wanting some revenue |
| **Referral** | Shop sends customers needing cheap bikes | Both benefit |
| **Overflow** | Co-op handles repairs shop doesn't want | Keeps customers in system |

**Building the Relationship:**
1. Visit in person, don't compete on price
2. Emphasize different markets (you serve those who can't afford new)
3. Offer to send customers their way for new bikes
4. Invite to events, acknowledge publicly
5. Some shop owners are bike advocates - find them

**What to Offer:**
- Referrals for customers who can afford new
- Volunteer recruitment (your volunteers may become their customers)
- Community event partnership
- Public recognition
- No direct price competition

### 4. Municipal Programs

Many cities have programs that generate bikes for donation.

**Sources:**
- City impound lots
- Parks department (abandoned in parks)
- Sanitation/waste department (curbside)
- Housing authority (abandoned at properties)
- Transit authority (abandoned at stations)

**How to Connect:**
- Attend city council meetings
- Contact sustainability/environmental office
- Reach out to individual departments
- Propose city-wide bike donation program

**City Partnership Benefits:**
- Addresses abandoned bike problem
- Supports city sustainability goals
- Provides constituent service
- Reduces waste stream
- Good PR for city officials

### 5. Community Donations

Individual donations from the public form the backbone of most co-op inventory.

**What You'll Receive:**
- Outgrown kids' bikes (excellent source)
- Bikes in garages/basements unused for years
- Deceased relative's bikes
- Bikes too expensive to repair commercially
- Impulse buys never used

**Making It Easy:**

| Method | Description | Pros/Cons |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| **Drop-off** | Bring to shop during hours | Simple, but limits donors |
| **Pickup** | You retrieve from donor | More bikes, costs time/fuel |
| **Events** | Collection days at locations | High volume, requires planning |
| **Partners** | Drop-off at bike shops, etc. | Extends reach, needs coordination |

**Donation Drives:**

Hold periodic donation events:
- **Earth Day** (April) - Environmental tie-in
- **Bike Month** (May) - Natural fit
- **Back-to-School** (August) - Kids bikes
- **Fall Cleanout** (October) - Garage cleaning season
- **Year-End** (December) - Tax deduction deadline

**Sample Donation Drive Flyer:**

```
🚲 BIKE DONATION DRIVE 🚲

[Date] | [Time]
[Location]

Clean out your garage and help your community!

WE ACCEPT:
✓ Bikes (any condition, any size)
✓ Bike parts and accessories
✓ Helmets (good condition only)
✓ Locks, lights, pumps

All donations support [Co-op Name]'s mission to
provide bikes and repair education to those in need.

TAX DEDUCTIBLE - Receipts provided!

Questions? [Contact info]

Can't make it? Drop off anytime at [address]
or schedule a pickup: [phone/email]
```

## Parts Sourcing

### New Parts Wholesale

| Category | Wholesale Sources | Notes |
|----------|-------------------|-------|
| **Tubes** | J&B Importers, QBP, BTI | Buy in bulk, major savings |
| **Tires** | Same as above | Stock common sizes |
| **Cables/housing** | Jagwire, bulk rolls | Cut to length |
| **Brake pads** | Kool-Stop, generic | Stock multiple types |
| **Chains** | KMC, generic | 6/7/8-speed most common |
| **Grips/tape** | Generic suppliers | Basic options sufficient |

**Setting Up Wholesale Accounts:**
- Most require business license/EIN
- Minimum orders often $100-500
- Net 30 terms common after established
- J&B Importers, QBP, BTI are major distributors

### Used Parts

**Harvesting from Donated Bikes:**

When a bike isn't worth refurbishing, harvest parts:

| Part | Shelf Life | Notes |
|------|------------|-------|
| Wheels | Long | If true, good tires |
| Tires | Medium | Check for dry rot |
| Tubes | Short | Often damaged, recycle |
| Seats | Long | Always needed |
| Seatposts | Long | Organize by diameter |
| Handlebars | Long | Various styles |
| Stems | Long | Organize by type |
| Brake levers | Long | Match to brake type |
| Shifters | Medium | Obsolete styles pile up |
| Derailleurs | Medium | Rear especially |
| Cranks | Long | Organize by type |
| Pedals | Long | Always needed |
| Chains | Short | Usually worn |
| Cables | Medium | If not frayed |

## Bike Intake & Assessment

### Intake Process

```
BIKE ARRIVES
     ↓
[INTAKE FORM]
- Date received
- Source (donor name or organization)
- Bike description (type, size, color, brand)
- Initial condition notes
- Receipt provided to donor
     ↓
[INITIAL ASSESSMENT]
- Rideable as-is?
- Worth refurbishing?
- Parts bike?
- Recycle?
     ↓
[ROUTE TO APPROPRIATE AREA]
```

### Assessment Criteria

**Keep & Refurbish (A-Grade):**
- Frame in good condition (no cracks, minimal rust)
- Correct size for common rider heights
- Repairable with reasonable parts investment
- Estimated value after repair: $75+

**Keep & Refurbish (B-Grade):**
- Functional but older/less desirable
- May need more work
- Good for earn-a-bike program
- Estimated value after repair: $30-75

**Parts Bike:**
- Frame damaged or wrong size
- Not worth repair investment
- Has salvageable parts
- Strip and organize parts

**Recycle:**
- Nothing salvageable
- Severe rust/damage throughout
- Take to metal recycler

## Partnership Agreements

### Key Elements

All formal partnerships should include:

1. **Parties** - Full legal names of both organizations
2. **Purpose** - What the partnership is for
3. **Terms** - What each party will do
4. **Duration** - Start date, end date, renewal terms
5. **Contacts** - Named individuals on each side
6. **Liability** - Who's responsible for what
7. **Termination** - How either party can end it
8. **Signatures** - Authorized representatives

### Sample Partnership Agreement

```
BICYCLE DONATION PARTNERSHIP AGREEMENT

This Agreement is entered into on [DATE] between:

DONOR: [Organization Name]
       [Address]
       Contact: [Name, Title, Phone, Email]

RECIPIENT: [Co-op Name]
           [Address]
           Contact: [Name, Title, Phone, Email]

PURPOSE:
[Donor] agrees to donate abandoned/unclaimed bicycles to
[Recipient] for community benefit programs including repair
education, earn-a-bike programs, and providing affordable
transportation.

DONOR AGREES TO:
1. Notify [Recipient] when bicycles are available for pickup
2. Provide reasonable access for pickup during business hours
3. Release bicycles that have met required holding period
4. Provide documentation of bikes released as needed

RECIPIENT AGREES TO:
1. Pick up bicycles within [X] days of notification
2. Provide proof of nonprofit status if requested
3. Use bicycles for community benefit purposes
4. Recognize [Donor] as a community partner
5. Provide annual summary of bikes received and impact

LIABILITY:
Recipient assumes all liability for bicycles upon pickup.
Recipient shall maintain appropriate insurance coverage.

DURATION:
This agreement begins on [DATE] and continues for one year,
renewing automatically unless either party provides 30 days
written notice of termination.

SIGNATURES:

_________________________  Date: __________
[Donor Representative]
[Title]

_________________________  Date: __________
[Recipient Representative]
[Title]
```

## Donation Receipts

### IRS Requirements

For tax-deductible donations:
- Donor must itemize deductions
- Co-op must be 501(c)(3) or fiscal sponsor must be
- Receipt required for donations over $250
- For vehicles/bikes valued over $500, special rules apply

### Fair Market Value Guide

| Item Condition | Kids Bike | Basic Adult | Quality Adult |
|----------------|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| Excellent | $30-50 | $50-100 | $100-300 |
| Good | $20-35 | $30-75 | $75-200 |
| Fair | $10-20 | $15-40 | $40-100 |
| Poor/Parts | $5-10 | $5-20 | $20-50 |

*Donors determine their own values; this is guidance only.*

## Building Long-Term Relationships

### Stewardship Best Practices

1. **Thank promptly** - Within 48 hours of donation
2. **Thank personally** - Call or handwritten note for major donors
3. **Report impact** - Annual summary of what donations accomplished
4. **Invite involvement** - Tours, events, volunteer opportunities
5. **Recognize publicly** - Social media, website, signage (with permission)
6. **Stay in touch** - Periodic updates, not just when you need something

### Partner Recognition Levels

| Level | Criteria | Recognition |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| **Friend** | 1-10 bikes/year | Thank-you letter, social media |
| **Supporter** | 11-50 bikes/year | Above + website listing |
| **Partner** | 51-100 bikes/year | Above + signage in shop |
| **Champion** | 100+ bikes/year | Above + annual event recognition |

### Annual Partner Report Template

```
ANNUAL PARTNER IMPACT REPORT

Dear [Partner Name],

RECOGNITION:
```

## Tracking & Metrics

### What to Track

**By Source:**
- Number of bikes received
- Condition distribution (A/B/Parts/Recycle)
- Pickup frequency
- Contact responsiveness

**Overall:**
- Total bikes received (monthly/annual)
- Source breakdown (% from each type)
- Conversion rate (% refurbished vs. parted vs. recycled)
- Parts inventory levels
- Cost per bike (if purchasing any)
